UTF-8 is an ASCII-preserving encoding method for Unicode <https://
en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode> (ISO 10646), the Universal Character Set
(UCS). The UCS encodes most of the world's writing systems in a single
character set, allowing you to mix languages and scripts within a
document without needing any tricks for switching character sets. This
web page is encoded directly in UTF-8. As shown HERE <https://
www.kermitproject.org/glass.html>, Columbia University's Kermit 95
<https://www.kermitproject.org/k95.html> terminal emulation software can
display UTF-8 plain text in Windows 95, 98, ME, NT, XP, Vista, or
Windows 7/8/10 when using a monospace Unicode font like Andale Mono WT J
<http://www.monotype.com/> or Everson Mono Terminal <http://
www.evertype.com/emono/>, or the lesser populated Courier New, Lucida
Console, or Andale Mono. C-Kermit <https://www.kermitproject.org/
ckermit.html> can handle it too, if you have a Unicode display <http://
www.cl.cam.ac.uk/~mgk25/unicode.html>. As many languages as are
representable in your font can be seen on the screen at the same time.
It started out as a kind of stress test for UTF-8 support in Web
browsers, which was spotty when this page was first created in 1996 but
which has become standard in all modern browsers. In fact only now
(2021, a quarter century later) every single "I can eat glass <#glass>"
entry is displayed correctly in my browser (Firefox on Windows 10),
including the Braille and the Gothic. Contrast with this screen shot
<https://www.kermitproject.org/courier.gif> from 2002, testing the UTF-8
support of our Kermit 95 <https://www.kermitproject.org/k95.html>
terminal emulator Microsoft's 2002 version of its Courier New font.
CLICK HERE <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode_font> for an up-to-
date survey of Unicode fonts.

    verb passive or reflexive)

    of the preceding vowel. Many thanks to the Óechtringen-

    Óechtringen-

    their relentless pursuit of the facts in this case. Conclusion: the
    accent almost certainly does not belong on this (or any other native
    German) word, but neither can it be dismissed as dirt on the page.

*Accented Cyrillic:*

/(This section contributed by Vladimir Marinov.)/

In Bulgarian it is desirable, customary, or in some cases required to
write accents over vowels. Unfortunately, no computer character sets
contain the full repertoire of accented Cyrillic letters. With Unicode,
however, it is possible to combine any Cyrillic letter with any
combining accent. The appearance of the result depends on the font and
the rendering engine. Here are two examples.

 1. Той видя бялата коса́ по главата и́ и ко́са на рамото и́, и ре́че да и́
    рече́: "Пара́та по́ па́ри от па́рата, не ща пари́!", но си поми́сли: "Хей,
    помисли́ си! А́ и́ река, а́ е скочила в тази река, която щеше да тече́, а
    не те́че."

*Tools Used to Create This Web Page:*
    The UTF8-aware Kermit 95 <https://www.kermitproject.org/k95.html>
    terminal emulator on Windows, to a Unix host with the EMACS <http://
    www.gnu.org/directory/emacs.html> text editor. Kermit 95 displays
    UTF-8 and also allows keyboard entry of arbitrary Unicode BMP
    characters as 4 hex digits, as shown HERE <https://
    www.kermitproject.org/glass.html>. Hex codes for Unicode values can
    be found through the Unicode Consortium Character Code Charts
    <https://www.unicode.org/charts/> index. When submissions arrive by
    email encoded in some other character set (Latin-1, Latin-2, KOI,
    various PC code pages, JEUC, etc), I use the TRANSLATE command of C-
    Kermit <https://www.kermitproject.org/ckermit.html> on a Unix host
    (e.g. Linux or NetBSD) to convert the character set to UTF-8 (I
    could also use Kermit 95 for this; it has the same TRANSLATE
    command). That's it -- no "Web authoring" tools, no locales, no
    "smart" anything. It's just plain text, nothing more. Editing in
    UTF-8 was rather tedious in times past but as as of about version 23
    or so, EMACS handles Unicode pretty well and works well with Kermit
    95 with its terminal character-set set to utf8.

*Commentary:*
    Date: Wed, 27 Feb 2002 13:21:59 +0100
    From: "Bruno DEDOMINICIS" <b.dedominicis@cite-sciences.fr>
    Subject: Je peux manger du verre, cela ne me fait pas mal.

    I just found out your website and it makes me feel like proposing an
    interpretation of the choice of this peculiar phrase.

    Glass is transparent and can hurt as everyone knows. The relation
    between people and civilisations is sometimes effusional and more
    often rude. The concept of breaking frontiers through globalization,
    in a way, is also an attempt to deny any difference. Isn't
    "transparency" the flag of modernity? Nothing should be hidden any
    more, authority is obsolete, and the new powers are supposed to
    reign through loving and smiling and no more through coercion...

    Eating glass without pain sounds like a very nice metaphor of this
    attempt. That is, frontiers should become glass transparent first,
    and be denied by incorporating them. On the reverse, it shows that
    through globalization, frontiers undergo a process of displacement,
    that is, when they are not any more speakable, they become repressed
    from the speech and are therefore incorporated and might become
    painful symptoms, as for example what happens when one tries to eat
    glass.

    The frontiers that used to separate bodies one from another tend to
    divide bodies from within and make them suffer.... The chosen phrase
    then appears as a denial of the symptom that might result from the
    destitution of traditional frontiers.

    Best,
    Bruno De Dominicis, Paris, France
